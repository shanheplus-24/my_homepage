Sustainable energy-water systems often behave like black boxes because performance is highly sensitive to ambient conditions, including dynamic solar path and moisture properties, and to the real-time thermodynamic states of materials and devices. Conventional physics-based models are therefore often too slow or too limited in scale for reliable prediction, and they offer limited support for inverse design.

We address this with data-driven machine learning that generalizes across climates. Physics-informed neural networks distill existing physics models into fast, accurate forward predictors, while conditional generative models and deep Q-networks can navigate high-dimensional, complex state spaces for inverse design. Embedding physical mechanisms and constraints into the learning pipeline enables accurate, scalable performance prediction and expands the feasible design space across devices and systems.
